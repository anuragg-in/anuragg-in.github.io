Tips

  • Avoid excess flour in the dough. A stickier consistency, especially after prolonged refrigeration, increases the likelihood of large, airy pockets in the crumb.
  • Use an aluminium foil on the base and the side of the tin baking pan. This will ensure that sides and the bottom of the bread does not get crusty due to direct contact with hot tin baking pan.



Baking soda vs. Baking powder vs. Yeast

Baking soda

Baking soda acts as a simple base and requires an acidic component, such as yogurt, lemon, or vinegar, to become activated. Its reaction with acid produces rapid gas bubbles, making it suitable for quick bake recipes like muffins, scones, and pancakes. Consider its use when time is a crucial factor, and an acidic ingredient is present in the recipe.

Baking powder

Baking powder is a combination of baking soda and a dry acid like cream of tartar. This allows for a double-acting leavening process, releasing CO2 gas bubbles during the addition of liquid and later, during heating. Ideal for recipes lacking additional acids, it only requires water and heat. Use baking powder when a prolonged leavening process is acceptable and no acidic ingredient is present.

Yeast

Yeast, a live organism, differs fundamentally from baking soda and baking powder. Leavening dough through a biological process and fermentation, yeast imparts a distinct flavor, including residual alcohol. Well-suited for bread-making, yeast requires more time but enhances the taste. Consider yeast when time is not a constraint, and a unique flavor profile is desired, especially in bread recipes.
